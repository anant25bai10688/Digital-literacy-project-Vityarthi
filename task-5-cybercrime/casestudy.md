# Case Study: Cyberbullying – The Invisible Harassment

## Type of Cybercrime
Cyberbullying

## What Is It?
Cyberbullying is the use of digital platforms — social media, messaging apps, online forums, or gaming platforms — to repeatedly harass, threaten, humiliate, or harm an individual. Unlike physical bullying, it can occur at any hour, follows the victim into their home, and can involve an audience of hundreds or thousands.

## How It Typically Happens (Step by Step)

1. **Targeting:** The perpetrator identifies a victim, usually someone in their peer group — a classmate, batchmate, or acquaintance.
2. **Initiation:** A hurtful comment, meme, or altered image is posted or circulated in a WhatsApp group, Instagram story, or public platform.
3. **Escalation:** Other users join in, share the content, or add their own comments, amplifying the harm.
4. **Isolation:** The victim is excluded from group chats, study groups, or social activities — both online and offline.
5. **Anonymity:** The bully may use fake or anonymous accounts to intensify the harassment while avoiding accountability.
6. **Sustained impact:** The content remains accessible even after deletion attempts, and screenshots continue to circulate.

## Realistic Scenario

Priya, a first-year engineering student, shared a candid photo of herself in a college WhatsApp group. A classmate saved the image, edited it using a filter app to make it appear unflattering, and shared it with a caption mocking her appearance in a group of 200 students. Within hours, the image was forwarded across multiple groups. Priya began receiving anonymous messages on Instagram ridiculing her. She withdrew from group activities, stopped attending lab sessions, and her academic performance declined significantly over the following month.

## Who Is Usually Targeted?
- Students new to college environments (first-year students are particularly vulnerable)
- Students who are socially isolated or perceived as different
- Individuals who speak out against peer groups or authority figures
- Anyone involved in interpersonal conflict that escalates online

## Consequences
- **Psychological:** Anxiety, depression, social withdrawal, sleep disruption, and in severe cases, self-harm ideation
- **Academic:** Drop in attendance, grades, and participation
- **Legal:** Under Section 67 of the IT Act, 2000, sharing obscene content electronically is punishable. Cyberbullying can also attract charges under IPC Sections 499 (defamation) and 507 (criminal intimidation by anonymous communication)
- **Reputational:** Both the victim and perpetrator can suffer lasting reputational consequences
